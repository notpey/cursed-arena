Design System v0.3 — February 2026

CURSED ARENA

A comprehensive design system built from three anchors: a purple-black void, a vivid cursed red, and a supernatural teal. Dark, menacing, editorially clean. Structured for Photoshop comp work with eventual development handoff.

Base: #17151c Text: #e4e6ef (Frost) Accent 1: #fa2742 (Cursed Red) Accent 2: #05d8bd (Teal) Theme: Dark / Menacing / Editorial

01 / Philosophy

DESIGN DIRECTION

Before any visual decisions, the team needs alignment on what Cursed Arena should feel like. These guardrails govern every design choice that follows.

The core tension: Cursed Arena lives at the intersection of menace and control. The world is supernatural and dangerous, but the player is in command. The UI should feel like a sealed barrier holding back chaos — clean edges, precise typography, controlled space — but underneath, color bleeds through. The teal hums with latent cursed energy. The red ruptures when action is demanded.

The emotional arc across screens: Not every screen should feel equally intense. The homepage is calm before the storm — dark, editorial, restrained. The gacha pull screen is where cursed energy erupts — teal glow, particles, dramatic motion. Battle UI is maximum tension — red accents, sharp contrast, urgent type. The collection screen is the trophy room — clean, reverent, organized. Design intensity maps to gameplay intensity.

Red vs. Teal — the semantic split: Red = player agency. "You do something." Battle CTAs, damage numbers, urgency, brand mark. Teal = system / supernatural. "Something is happening." Cursed energy, gacha mechanics, buff/heal, progression, domain expansions. This is the most important design rule in the system. If both colors appear simultaneously, the player should feel the tension between action and the unknown.

2026 Trend

Anti-sterility / "Imperfect by Design": The dominant 2026 direction actively rejects algorithmic neutrality. Fonts should have historical friction and visible personality. Backgrounds should have atmosphere. The human hand should be visible in the design even if AI assisted production. For Cursed Arena: avoid anything that looks auto-generated. Your display type should have character, not geometric perfection.

2026 Trend

Cinematic gradients as light sources: Gradients in 2026 function as directional lighting, not decoration. A red vignette from the left signals danger. A teal glow emanating from behind a character card suggests cursed energy. Position gradients off-center, let them bleed past edges. In Photoshop, build these as separate screen/soft-light layers at 5–15% opacity.

2026 Trend

Micro-delight over macro-motion: The 2026 motion consensus favors small, purposeful moments: a button that gives a subtle bounce on press, a progress bar that slightly overshoots before settling. One well-choreographed page load with staggered reveals creates more impact than scattered effects. Annotate these moments on your comps — motion is part of the design.

2026 Trend

Kinetic / variable typography: Variable fonts allow weight, width, and optical size to shift dynamically on scroll, hover, or state change. For Cursed Arena, consider a variable display face where weight animates heavier during battle and lighter during menus. Show both states side by side in your comps as a motion spec.

Photoshop layer stack: (1) Solid fill #17151c. (2) Grain: fractal noise, mono, 3–5% opacity, overlay. (3) Vignettes: radial gradients, accent color to transparent, 8–15% opacity, screen blend. Key specs for annotation:

Button press: translateY(-1px) on hover (200ms expo-out), glow fades in. Active: translateY(0), darken, glow off (immediate).

Card hover: translateY(-2px) + border shift + shadow (200ms). Return: 350ms (slower = "reluctant release").

Progress bar: Fills from 0 on load (600ms expo-out, 200ms delay). Slight overshoot via back-out for bars above 80%.

Page load stagger: Sidebar (0ms, 350ms) → Hero (100ms, 600ms) → Cards stagger (200/280/360ms, 350ms each). Each: opacity 0→1 + translateY(12px→0).

Gacha reveal: 900ms dramatic. Screen darkens, teal vignette intensifies, card scales 0.8→1.0 with spring overshoot. Teal glow radiates outward. This is the emotional climax — spend the animation budget here.

Teal has excellent contrast. #05d8bd against #17151c hits ~8.5:1 — well above AAA. This is a usability advantage: teal can be used for smaller text (links, data highlights, inline labels) where red would fail at small sizes. Use teal for information density; reserve red for focal moments.

10 / Guardrails

DO / DON'T

Do

Use red for exactly one CTA per viewport. Let teal handle navigation indicators, progress, data highlights, and secondary actions. Red is the wound; teal is the pulse.

Don't

Use red and teal at equal frequency. If both appear everywhere with the same visual weight, the semantic split collapses and your UI has two primary colors fighting for attention.

Do

Use atmospheric gradients as directional light — off-center, purpose-driven, subtle. Teal glows from the right, red bleeds from the left. Create visual tension through opposing light sources.

Don't

Place a red vignette and a teal vignette both centered on the same element. Opposing colors should come from opposing directions. Same origin = visual mud.

Do

Use dark text (#17151c) on teal buttons. The high contrast ratio (8.5:1) makes it crisp. Use white text on red buttons where the ratio is tighter (4.6:1).

Don't

Use white text on teal. It works but wastes contrast headroom. Dark-on-teal is sharper and more distinctive — it also visually differentiates teal buttons from red ones at a glance.

Do

Keep the purple undertone in your background scale. The darkness itself has a color identity that ties into the supernatural theme.

Don't

Drift to neutral gray in your elevation steps. Once you break the purple undertone, the backgrounds lose their cohesion with the accent palette and everything looks like a generic dark theme.

11 / Workflow

PHOTOSHOP SETUP

Step 1 — Canvas: 1440 x 900 desktop. 375 x 812 mobile. 1024 x 768 tablet. 1x. Background fill #17151c.

Step 2 — Swatches: Import every hex as a swatch group. Name with slashes: "BG / Void", "BG / Base", "Red / Glow", "Red / Primary", "Teal / Glow", "Teal / Primary", "Text / Primary", etc.

Step 3 — Grid: 8px grid (Preferences → Guides, Grid & Slices). 12-column layout: 280px sidebar + 24px gutter + main area with 24px column gutters. Snap to Grid on.

Step 4 — Atmosphere: Three base layers: (1) Solid #17151c. (2) "Atmosphere" group at 8% opacity — vignettes + grain. (3) Empty "Content" group. This gives you depth before drawing a single UI element.

Step 5 — Smart objects: Build one card, one button, one progress bar, one tag as smart objects. Duplicate from masters. Prevents drift where each card ends up 2px different.

Step 6 — Annotations: Locked group at top of layer stack for motion specs, spacing values, and interaction states. Hide for export, show for handoff.

The squint test: Zoom to 25%, squint. You should see: nav bar, one red CTA, teal accents threading through data/progress, content hierarchy large→small. If you see two pools of competing color, the red/teal balance is off. If you see monochrome gray, your accents aren't deployed enough.
